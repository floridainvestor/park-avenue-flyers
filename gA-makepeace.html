She Did Everything Right

She had saved. Enough for a down payment, she thought. Enough to stop renting and start owning — finally. A real home. Something stable. Something hers.

She called the first bank. They said she'd need a 680 score and 20% down. The second bank said the same thing, with a longer form. The third did not call back. She sat with the phone in her hand for a long moment. Then she put it down.

She was not reckless. She was not irresponsible. She had worked thirty years, paid her taxes, raised her children. And still the door was closed. Not slammed — just quietly, firmly closed. The banks did not hate her. They simply did not see her.

What Happens If Nothing Changes

She goes back to the landlord next month. Rent goes up again — it always does. In Sanford right now, a one-bedroom apartment runs $1,650 to $1,900 a month on a good day. (Zillow, Q1 2026.) She pays it. Keeps paying it. The landlord builds equity. She builds nothing. Five years from now, ten years from now — she is still paying, still renting, still hoping the next place doesn't raise rates again. That is the math of doing nothing.
